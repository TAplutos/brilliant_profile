EClearly the limit is somewhere between 0 and 1. If you graph it, you can practically guess that the answer is .5, but here's the algebraic proof.

Begin by working with the floor function half of the equation. It can be observed that for all positive integer values of nnn, ⌊n2+n+1⌋=n\left\lfloor\sqrt{n^2+n+1}\right\rfloor=n⌊n2+n+1​⌋=n. Thus our function we are evaluating is lim⁡n→∞n2+n+1−n\displaystyle \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} \sqrt{n^2+n+1}-nn→∞lim​n2+n+1​−n.

Next, Consider the perfect square polynomials n2n^2n2 and n2+2n+1n^2+2n+1n2+2n+1 and "average" the two. n2+n2+2n+12=n2+n+12\dfrac{n^2+n^2+2n+1}{2}=n^2+n+\frac{1}{2}2n2+n2+2n+1​=n2+n+21​. The radical part of the function we are evaluating is the average of two perfect squares (we can ignore the difference in constants because as nnn achieves large enough values, the constant is negligible). So taking the average of our functions we have

n2−n=0\sqrt{n^2}-n=0n2​−n=0

n2+2n+1−n=1\sqrt{n^2+2n+1}-n=1n2+2n+1​−n=1
