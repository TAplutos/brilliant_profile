I made this problem to prove that this factoring trick can be used for polys of all degrees.

Let's look at the constant term. Using the identity, ϕn=ϕn−1+ϕn−2\phi^n=\phi^{n-1}+\phi^{n-2}ϕn=ϕn−1+ϕn−2.

210+315ϕ=105+105ϕ+105ϕ+105+105ϕ210+315\phi=105+105\phi+105\phi+105+105\phi210+315ϕ=105+105ϕ+105ϕ+105+105ϕ

⟹105ϕ2+105ϕ2+105ϕ=105ϕ2+105ϕ3=105ϕ4\Longrightarrow 105\phi^2+105\phi^2+105\phi=105\phi^2+105\phi^3=105\phi^4⟹105ϕ2+105ϕ2+105ϕ=105ϕ2+105ϕ3=105ϕ4

Now we have

105ϕ4+76iϕ3x+34ϕ2x2+4iϕx3+x4=0105\phi^4+76i\phi^3x+34\phi^2x^2+4i\phi x^3+x^4=0105ϕ4+76iϕ3x+34ϕ2x2+4iϕx3+x4=0

Using this. We extract ϕ\phiϕ and iii inversely with xxx (aka, as the power of xxx decreases, we factor out larger and larger powers of iii and ϕ\phiϕ) and are left with

105−76x−34x2+4x3+x4=0105-76x-34x^2+4x^3+x^4=0105−76x−34x2+4x3+x4=0

Remainder theorem tells us that x=1x=1x=1 is a root.

(x−1)(x3+5x2−29x−105)=0(x-1)(x^3+5x^2-29x-105)=0(x−1)(x3+5x2−29x−105)=0

Once again, remainder theorem/rational roots tells us that x=5x=5x=5 is a root.

(x−1)(x−5)(x2+10x+21)=0(x-1)(x-5)(x^2+10x+21)=0(x−1)(x−5)(x2+10x+21)=0

(x−1)(x−5)(x+3)(x+7)=0(x-1)(x-5)(x+3)(x+7)=0(x−1)(x−5)(x+3)(x+7)=0

Factoring back in the iii and ϕ\phiϕ

(x−iϕ)(x−5iϕ)(x+3iϕ)(x+7iϕ)=0(x-i\phi)(x-5i\phi)(x+3i\phi)(x+7i\phi)=0(x−iϕ)(x−5iϕ)(x+3iϕ)(x+7iϕ)=0

Thus a1=iϕ, a2=−3iϕ, a3=5iϕ, a4=−7iϕa_1=i\phi,~a_2=-3i\phi,~a_3=5i\phi,~a_4=-7i\phia1​=iϕ, a2​=−3iϕ, a3​=5iϕ, a4​=−7iϕ and
