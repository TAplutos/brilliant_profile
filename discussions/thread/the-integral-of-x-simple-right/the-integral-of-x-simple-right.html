The integral of x... Simple right?

Today, my friend asked me when to use trig sub in integration. Sarcastically, I said, "always" to which he replied, "Really? What about when you integrate x." So I thought for a second and this is what resulted.

∫x dx\displaystyle\int x~dx∫x dx

x=sin⁡(u)x=\sin(u)x=sin(u)

dx=cos⁡(u) dudx=\cos(u)~dudx=cos(u) du

∫cos⁡(u)sin⁡(u) du=12∫sin⁡(2u) du\displaystyle\int \cos(u)\sin(u)~du=\frac{1}{2}\displaystyle\int \sin(2u)~du∫cos(u)sin(u) du=21​∫sin(2u) du

w=2uw=2uw=2u

14∫sin⁡(w) dw=−14cos⁡(2u)=−14(1−2sin⁡2(u))\frac{1}{4}\displaystyle\int \sin(w) ~dw=-\frac{1}{4} \cos(2u)=-\frac{1}{4}(1-2\sin^2(u))41​∫sin(w) dw=−41​cos(2u)=−41​(1−2sin2(u))

−14(1−2x2)=−14+x22+c-\frac{1}{4}(1-2x^2)=-\frac{1}{4}+\frac{x^2}{2}+c−41​(1−2x2)=−41​+2x2​+c

Which, because of the constant of integration, is equivalent to x22+c\frac{x^2}{2}+c2x2​+c

Moral of the story: always use trig sub.
