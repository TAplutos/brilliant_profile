Thales' Theorem

Image Credits: Wikipedia

Thales' Theorem is a simple yet important geometrical result which describes the relationship between inscribed right angles and circles. A special case of the inscribed angle theorem, Thales' Theorem is as follows:

Let AAA and CCC be two points on the circumference of some circle SSS such that AC‾\overline{AC}AC is a diameter of SSS. If a point BBB is placed anywhere on the circumference of SSS (other than AAA or CCC), then ∠ABC\angle ABC∠ABC will always be a right angle.

Using the inscribed angle theorem, it also follows that the reverse of this statement holds. Given any 3 points A,B,CA,B,CA,B,C on the circumference of SSS such that ∠ABC=90∘\angle ABC=90^{\circ}∠ABC=90∘, AC‾\overline{AC}AC must be a diameter of the circle. We now present two different approaches to proving Thales'.

A proof via inscribed angle theorem.

Consider a circle SSS with center OOO and two diametrically opposite points A,CA,CA,C on the circumference ofSSS. Since points A,C,OA,C,OA,C,O are all co-linear, it follows that ∠AOC=180∘\angle AOC=180^{\circ}∠AOC=180∘.

By the inscribed angle theorem, we know that any angle θ\thetaθ inscribed in SSS will be half of the central angle which subtends the same arc on SSS. In other words, if a point BBB is placed on the circumference of SSS, then angle ∠ABC\angle ABC∠ABC will be half of angle ∠AOC\angle AOC∠AOC. Since we have that ∠AOC=180∘\angle AOC=180^{\circ}∠AOC=180∘, it follows that ∠ABC=90∘\angle ABC=90^{\circ}∠ABC=90∘

Thales' theorem has many elegant applications to pure geometry. However, since Thales' often arises in coordinate geometry, let's take a look at a proof of Thales' which uses just that.

A proof via coordinate geometry.

Consider a circle SSS of radius rrr located at the origin. Notice that SSS is described by the equation x2+y2=r2x^2+y^2=r^2x2+y2=r2. Consider the two diametrically opposite points (−r,0)(-r,0)(−r,0) and (r,0)(r,0)(r,0) located at the intersection of SSS with the xxx-axis. Place a third point anywhere on the upper half of the circumference of SSS.

Pythagoras' theorem guarantees that in a triangle with side lengths a,b,ca,b,ca,b,c, with ccc being the longest side, a2+b2=c2a^2+b^2=c^2a2+b2=c2 if and only if the triangle is a right triangle. Therefore, we will show that θ=90∘\theta=90^{\circ}θ=90∘ in the diagram to the right by showing that a2+b2=c2a^2+b^2=c^2a2+b2=c2.

Using Pythagoras' theorem, we can determine the legnths of each side as follows

a2=(x−(−r))2+(y−0)2=x2+2xr+r2+y2b2=(x−r)2+(y−0)2=x2−2xr+r2+y2c2=(r−(−r))2=4r2\begin{aligned} a^2&=(x-(-r))^2+(y-0)^2=x^2+2xr+r^2+y^2\\b^2&=(x-r)^2+(y-0)^2=x^2-2xr+r^2+y^2\\c^2&=(r-(-r))^2=4r^2\end{aligned}a2b2c2​=(x−(−r))2+(y−0)2=x2+2xr+r2+y2=(x−r)2+(y−0)2=x2−2xr+r2+y2=(r−(−r))2=4r2​

Next, observe that

a2+b2=(x2+2xr+r2+y2)+(x2−2xr+r2+y2)=2x2+2y2+2r2=2(x2+y2)+2r2a^2+b^2=(x^2+2xr+r^2+y^2)+(x^2-2xr+r^2+y^2)=2x^2+2y^2+2r^2=2(x^2+y^2)+2r^2a2+b2=(x2+2xr+r2+y2)+(x2−2xr+r2+y2)=2x2+2y2+2r2=2(x2+y2)+2r2

Recall that since the point (x,y)(x,y)(x,y) was chosen on the circumference of SSS, it must satisfy x2+y2=r2x^2+y^2=r^2x2+y2=r2. Using this to simplify the equation above:

a2+b2=2(x2+y2)+2r2=2(r2)+2r2=4r2=c2a^2+b^2=2(x^2+y^2)+2r^2=2(r^2)+2r^2=4r^2=c^2a2+b2=2(x2+y2)+2r2=2(r2)+2r2=4r2=c2

Since we have shown that a2+b2=c2a^2+b^2=c^2a2+b2=c2, we have also proved that θ=90∘\theta=90^{\circ}θ=90∘ which finishes our proof of Thales'.

There are many other ways to prove Thales', some using pure geometry, some using trigonometry, and some even using complex numbers! We encourage you to explore and try to come up with your own unique proof of Thales'. If you do manage to come up with your own proof, great! Just don't get too excited and sacrifice an ox (see entry "Thales' theorems" on this page for more details).

Here are a couple problems centered around Thales' for practice. The first few aren't too bad, but be careful, the last one is quite tricky!

Circle SSS is placed in the 2D-plane with radius 333 and its center OOO placed on the yyy-axis. SSS intersects the yyy-axis at both the point GGG and the origin (labeled FFF).

Circle TTT is then drawn with twice the radius of circle SSS such that TTT also intersects FFF and GGG.

If circle TTT intersects the xxx-axis at points XXX and GGG, find the length of segment FX‾\overline{FX}FX.



Since the xxx-axis and yyy-axis are perpendicular, ∠XFG=90∘\angle XFG=90^{\circ}∠XFG=90∘. Combined with the fact that ΔXFG\Delta XFGΔXFG is inscribed in TTT, Thales' theorem tells us that XG‾\overline{XG}XG is a diameter of TTT.

The problem is finished by using Pythagorean theorem on ΔXFG\Delta XFGΔXFG to get ∣XF‾∣=122−62=108|\overline{XF}|=\sqrt{12^2-6^2}=\sqrt{108}∣XF∣=122−62​=108​.

Circles SSS and TTT are co-centric at point OOO with circle SSS inside circle TTT. Line segment BA‾\overline{BA}BA is a cord of circle TTT and is tangent to circle SSS at point DDD. Line segment BC‾\overline{BC}BC is a cord of circle TTT and is perpendicular to segment BABABA.

If BC‾\overline{BC}BC has length 1 and BA‾\overline{BA}BA has length 2, find the radius of the inner circle SSS



Use the Pythagorean theorem on ΔOAD\Delta OADΔOAD to find OD‾\overline{OD}OD once you've found the length of OA‾\overline{OA}OA.



By Pythagorean Theorem, ∣AC‾∣=5|\overline{AC}|=\sqrt{5}∣AC∣=5​. Since ∠ABC=90∘\angle ABC=90^{\circ}∠ABC=90∘, Thales' guarantees that AC‾\overline{AC}AC is a diameter of the outer circle. Since both circles share the same center OOO, we know that AC‾\overline{AC}AC passes through the center of both circles. Therefore, ∣OA‾∣=52|\overline{OA}|=\frac{\sqrt{5}}{2}∣OA∣=25​​ since it is a radius of the outer circle.

By symmetry, we know that DDD is the midpoint of AB‾\overline{AB}AB and therefore ∣AD‾∣=1|\overline{AD}|=1∣AD∣=1.

Finally, using Pythagorean theorem on ΔOAD\Delta OADΔOAD, we get ∣OD‾∣=12|\overline{OD}|=\frac{1}{2}∣OD∣=21​.
