Factoring by Extraction

Today, I will discuss how to factor polynomials with large coefficients such as 3x2+10x−1003x^2+10x-1003x2+10x−100 with ease. I know that this is a long note but I feel that it is worth reading everything including the generalized form at the bottom except for the proof (unless you want to)

While sitting in my math class, I discovered a trick to factoring second degree polynomial's with large or irrational second and third coefficients. For example, try factoring 3x2+10x−10003x^2+10x-10003x2+10x−1000. It's relatively simple to factor it to (3x−50)(x+20)(3x-50)(x+20)(3x−50)(x+20) but that would take a little while or at least longer than the way that I'm about to discuss.

We begin with the expression 3x2+10x−10003x^2+10x-10003x2+10x−1000. Then, we devide the second coefficient by 10 and the third by 100 and we are left with the expression 3x2+x−103x^2+x-103x2+x−10 which we can easily factor to (3x−5)(x+2)(3x-5)(x+2)(3x−5)(x+2). Finally, we multiply the second term in each coefficient by 10 and we have (3x−50)(x+20)(3x-50)(x+20)(3x−50)(x+20). Looks familiar doesn't it? Basically, what I have done is I divided the second coefficient by any\color{#20A900}{\text{Basically, what I have done is I divided the second coefficient by any}}Basically, what I have done is I divided the second coefficient by any one of its factors (in this case 10) and divided the third coefficient by the\color{#20A900}{\text{one of its factors (in this case 10) and divided the third coefficient by the}}one of its factors (in this case 10) and divided the third coefficient by the square of that factor while leaving the first untouched.\color{#20A900}{\text{square of that factor while leaving the first untouched.}}square of that factor while leaving the first untouched.

--This method applies to irrational and imaginary coefficients as well. For example take 4x2+82x+84x^2+8\sqrt2x+84x2+82​x+8. We can factor out 222\sqrt222​ from the second coefficient and 8 from the third and are left with 4x2+4x+1⇒(2x+1)2⇒(2x+22)24x^2+4x+1\Rightarrow(2x+1)^2\Rightarrow(2x+2\sqrt2)^24x2+4x+1⇒(2x+1)2⇒(2x+22​)2

--This also works in reverse from the first coefficient. Say we have 25x2−60x+3625x^2-60x+3625x2−60x+36. We can factor out 5 from the middle coefficient and 25 from the first and are left with x2−12x+36x^2-12x+36x2−12x+36. Next, we can factor out a 6 from the second coefficient and 36 from the final coefficient and are left with x2−2x+1⇒(x−1)2x^2-2x+1\Rightarrow (x-1)^2x2−2x+1⇒(x−1)2. Finally, we factor back in 5 to the first coefficient and a 6 into the last one to get (5x−6)2(5x-6)^2(5x−6)2

PROOF:

--Now, we shall prove why this works.

--Take the general expression ax2+bx+cax^2+bx+cax2+bx+c which can be factored into (dx+e)(fx+g)(dx+e)(fx+g)(dx+e)(fx+g). This means that a=df, b=dg+ef, and c=eg. The last step of our method requires us to multiply both second coefficients in our binomials by n (n being the number that we factored out of b). So our expression becomes (dx+en)(fx+gn)(dx+\frac{e}{n})(fx+\frac{g}{n})(dx+ne​)(fx+ng​) which means that a=df,b=dg+efn,c=egn2a=df, b=\frac{dg+ef}{n}, c=\frac{eg}{n^2}a=df,b=ndg+ef​,c=n2eg​. This is why we factor out n and n^2 from the second and third coefficients respectively.

--This rule can actually apply to polynomials of all degree. Unfortunately, the higher the degree of the polynomial, the lower the chances are that this will work. But say we have f(x) with degree n. Just not necessarily with integer or real coefficients. Next we assume that all coefficients after the second (inclusive), are divisible by rtr^trt where t represents the location of r relative to the second coefficient. This means that we have ∏t=0n(atx+btr)\displaystyle\prod_{t=0}^n (a_tx+b_tr)t=0∏n​(at​x+bt​r) which is equivalent to ∑t=0n(rn−t(ptxt))\displaystyle\sum_{t=0}^n (r^{n-t}(p_tx^t))t=0∑n​(rn−t(pt​xt)) Upon expansion, we get pnxn+r(pn−1xn−1)+r2(pn−2xn−2)+r3(pn−3xn−3)...rn−1(p1x1)+rn(p0)p_nx^n+r(p_{n-1}x^{n-1})+r^2(p_{n-2}x^{n-2})+r^3(p_{n-3}x^{n-3})...r^{n-1}(p_1x^1)+r^n(p_0)pn​xn+r(pn−1​xn−1)+r2(pn−2​xn−2)+r3(pn−3​xn−3)...rn−1(p1​x1)+rn(p0​). As we can see, this method works whenever every coefficient after the first is divisible by rtr^trt.

--A useful method for seeing if this works is by prime factoring every coefficient after the second and looking for a common term. That has multiple powers throughout. Say we have x4−3x3−63x2+27x+486x^4-3x^3-63x^2+27x+486x4−3x3−63x2+27x+486. First we prime factor the numbers to get 3:3 | 63:3^2,7 | 27:3^3 | 486: 3^5,2. As we can see, they share 3 in increasing powers. Therefore, we can eliminate 3 in increasing powers from each coefficient and are left with

(x4−x3−7x2+x+6)⇒x3(x−1)−(7x+6)(x−1)(x^4-x^3-7x^2+x+6)\Rightarrow x^3(x-1)-(7x+6)(x-1)(x4−x3−7x2+x+6)⇒x3(x−1)−(7x+6)(x−1)

(x−3)(x+2)(x+1)(x−1)(x-3)(x+2)(x+1)(x-1)(x−3)(x+2)(x+1)(x−1).

In general:

A cubic can always be represented by the form a(x−r1)(x−r2)(x−r3)a(x-r_1)(x-r_2)(x-r_3)a(x−r1​)(x−r2​)(x−r3​) (this is its factored form). Let's also say that the general form for the above factored cubic is ax3+bx2+cx+dax^3+bx^2+cx+dax3+bx2+cx+d.

Let's say that a certain cubic can be represented as a(x−kr1)(x−kr2)(x−kr3)a(x-kr_1)(x-kr_2)(x-kr_3)a(x−kr1​)(x−kr2​)(x−kr3​) for some constant kkk. Then, when you expand its factored form to get its general form, you get ax3+kbx2+k2cx+k3dax^3+kbx^2+k^2cx+k^3dax3+kbx2+k2cx+k3d and we're done.

This easily generalizes to all polynomials.
